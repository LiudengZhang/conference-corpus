## Thesis

The closing keynote of GBCC2025 is a meta-talk, not a methods-talk. The "Conquest of Abundance" framing — riffing on Feyerabend's posthumous book of the same title — flips the usual data-deluge narrative: the problem is no longer scarcity of sequence, it is the *abundance* itself, and specifically who gets to use it. The argument is almost certainly that infrastructure, training, and equity of access — not the next algorithm — are what determine whether a time of genomic plenty actually compounds into useful biology, and that this is a community-design question as much as a technical one.

## Speaker context

Williams works at the CSHL DNA Learning Center, the longest-running large-scale genomics-education effort in the country, and has been a consistent voice on training, access, and equity in computational biology — including substantial involvement in [AnVIL](https://anvilproject.org/) training and the broader "cloud-genomics for everyone" agenda. Closing GBCC2025 with him — after Pond's in-the-browser-compute keynote and Soneson's classes-to-community keynote — is a deliberate sequencing: tools, then ecosystem, then *who can actually pick the tools up*. The CSHL venue makes the choice doubly fitting; the DNA Learning Center is on the same grounds as the meeting.
